Inti · Mate

Introduction

This website is a complement to the Design with Data report. It summarises the five components of intimacy, and presents a prototype of the audiovisual interactions intended for the physical exhibit.

Summary of components for intimacy

Commitment: the desire to permanently remain with the partner, which is viewed as the foundation that provides opportunities for the other intimacy dimensions to develop

Cognitive Closeness: the depth of awareness individuals have of their partner's cognitive world and the exchanges of cognitions they share (e.g., values, goals, and other information)

Affective Closeness: refers to the depth of awareness individuals have about their partner's emotional world and the exchanges of emotions they share, (e.g., feelings of caring and compassion)

Physical closeness: refers to the extent of shared physical encounters (ranging from proximity to sexuality) as well as to the physiological arousal state experienced toward the partner at each level of physical encounter

Mutuality: An exchange or mutual interaction (characterizing intimacy as a process that occurs between individuals)

Taken from Moss, B., & Schwebel, A. (1993). Defining Intimacy in Romantic Relationships. Family Relations, 42(1), 31-37. doi:10.2307/58491

Prototype

Below is a prototype of the exhibit, showcasing the imagined interaction between user inputs and the audiovisual output. The red and green knobs represent the two people using the exhibit; in the physical exhibit, these knobs would be placed on opposite sides of the wall, and people would be able to cycle through the questions independently of each other. The visual output shows what the projection in the physical exhibit would be displaying. Here's how to explore the prototype:

  • To start, click on the top-right SOUND button. You can use it to mute the prototype as well
  • Click and drag your mouse up or down to change the value of a knob. A knob turned to the left means you care little about that category, while a knob turned to the right means you care a lot about that category
  • Use the left and right buttons to navigate between questions
  • The first three knobs affect the audio, the other two affect the visuals; the audio also affects the visuals by itself

The prototype uses the Rotating Knobs library by Miles Decoster, and the Time Domain Oscilloscope by Jason Sigal. If the sound doesn't seem to work, please try opening the website on another browser.

Matching mechanic

The prototype starts in a state where both people's answers are similar, and the knobs have identical positions; they are considered very compatible. This is reflected in the audiovisual output, through having a more colourful visualisation with a thicker oscilloscope line, and the various sound effects sounding good together. If the answers of the two people do not match at all, they are considered incompatible. The visualisation is darker, the line is thinner, and the audio pans on the left/right while the sounds conflict with each other, to show the distance between their answers. As the knobs are interacted with, the changes in audiovisual output can be seen and heard in real-time, and reflect the level of agreement of the two people's answers.
